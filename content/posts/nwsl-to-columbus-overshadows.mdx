Today, the NWSL, City of Columbus and new ownership group (Haslam Sports Group, Nationwide, The Edwards Family) announced the awarding of the 18th expansion team to Columbus with a 205 million (!!!) bid to secure the team. What should have been an exciting day for the city, women's athletes, and soccer fans alike has quickly crumbled into bad community relationships from the get go; touting back door deals, a scramble for approval, and conflicted feelings for many who have been wishing for a NWSL team for the city since the leagues beginning in 2013.

I personally have _really strong feels_ about this one. I grew up in Columbus. I quite literally, as a little girl, played from ages 5-18 in Columbus and its suburbs before going off to play college ball. The Columbus Crew was my formative professional soccer model growing up -- we went to plenty of Ohio State women's soccer games but there was never truly a regular model for girls that said _you can do this professionally too_. The Crew was all we had.

My parents attended the first Crew ever game at Ohio Stadium and we regularly went to games when we weren't traveling for my own. In 2017 when Anthony Precourt announced that they would be moving the Crew to Austin, I supported the #SaveTheCrew movement as best I could while in my freshmen year of undergrad. In fact, the same year Dr. Pete literally shoved needles in my calves to test for compartment syndrome. There's not a single part of my life where I can't point back to some ongoing relationship with the organization that is Columbus Crew soccer -- and there's always been an aching to see people more like me doing this professionally.

Bear with me as I attempt to piece together my observations and thoughts regarding the compressed timeline we saw here with the #NWSLToCBus. I'm seeing a lot of varying opinions and takes from people who know MLS (& Crew) and NWSL separately, painting things to be very black and white and in our world where that is rarely ever the case. I'm not really here to make an argument or paint myself as an expert on either, rather provide my own commentary on what is likely going to be one of the more confusing expansion awards in the NWSL's history.

# The "Beginning" of Columbus

Let's rewind (...a little bit). Just in March, the ["IgniteHer"](https://www.columbussports.org/press-releases/post/columbus-leaders-launch-igniteher-columbus/ "Columbus Leaders Launch IgniteHER Columbus, a Citywide Women’s Sports Initiative") initiative was launched through the Columbus Sports Commission. The initiative is doing some very real things, like

- starting a Women in Sports and Events chapter
- providing research grants
- establishing a network for women collegiate athletes that transition into business

which are all _arguably good things_ for the community. While officially launched in March, the idea was actually announced on _April 16th, 2025_ during the 2025 State of the City Address. And now -- a little over year later the officially announcement awarding the City a team. While this isn't really an official step in the process that awarded Columbus the team, it makes it really easy to wonder what conversations were going on behind closed doors.

A few other things worth pointing out:

- Mayor Ginther's wife, Shannon Ginther, chairs the Women's Commission. She served on a panel during the IgniteHER launch panel with Mary Sherpo, the Crew's President of Business Operations.
- Columbus City Council and Franklin County commissioners approved a combined [$1 million "lifeline" grant ($500,000 from each)](https://www.aol.com/articles/columbus-council-narrowly-approves-500-005756608.html "Columbus Council approves $500,000 for Columbus Fury") in late 2025 to support the Columbus Fury professional volleyball team, which is the second precedent of providing public funds for privately owned teams.
  - The Crew's new stadium in 2019 (more on that later) was the first
  - As far as I can tell, the Fury is the first instance of a professional women's team receiving public funds for operational costs
- January 2026 the NWSL announced the Challenge Cup (Gotham FC vs. Kansas City Current) would be played at ScottsMiracle-Gro Field on June 26

While overall while I think of an initiative promoting women's sports and investment in it as a good thing, IgniteHER also lays the moral groundwork and framing for investment into this NWSL team -- which comes back into play later on when discussing McCoy park.

# The Bad Deal

Around February 2026, HSG publicly confirmed in an article that broke from ESPN that they were exploring an NWSL expansion bid alongside Nationwide and the Edwards family. As someone actively involved in the Columbus soccer communities, my feeds were filled with excitement. I was filled with excitement. There was (and I hope is) a genuine excitement for a women's team. This excitement blossomed into a grassroots petition started by the same people behind Save the Crew, because Columbus soccer fans know better than most how to organize passionately behind an idea. 

This all is happening around the same time as [reports breaking on the proposed McCoy Park development](https://columbusunderground.com/haslam-sports-group-submits-plans-to-city-for-soccer-training-facility-bw1/) and the 2026 State of the City address confirming the NWSL bid and its ties to the IgniteHER initiative in March. Later in March, WOSU goes on to report on the entire $50 million plan ($25 million each between the City and County) and Councilmember Day-Achauer goes on record saying [members "were briefed on a plan a few weeks ago"](https://www.wosu.org/sports/2026-03-24/columbus-city-council-franklin-county-weigh-50-million-request-to-help-attract-a-nwsl-team "Columbus City Council, Franklin County weigh $50 million request to help attract a NWSL team"). The first iteration of the deal committed to

* $25 million from Columbus (matched by $25M from Franklin County = $50M total)
* Training facility built at McCoy Park on the southwest side
* Upgrades to ScottsMiracle-Gro Field (SMG Field from here out, because I'm lazy) to accommodate the women's team
* A 28-acre McCoy Park land lease to the Confluence Community Authority — a special government entity established by the city and county to own the Crew's stadium
* $12 million private donation from the ownership group over 12 years, focused on early childhood education, workforce development, and food insecurity

More specifically this first iteration of the deal was funded by general tax payer dollars, which politically is very difficult to defend.

# The NWSL Move to Rolling Expansion

I want to side track for a moment, because at this point in time we are starting to see a lot of language regarding the compressed timeline of this bid, and I think part of that is due to the changing expansion model of the NWSL. Up until 2024, the NWSL ran with a fairly structured model, holding bidding periods with deadlines. Bay FC and Boston Legacy were both announced early 2023, then Denver the winner between Denver, Cincinnati and Cleveland on (roughly) the same timeline in January 2025. Essentially, a 12-18ish month long process with public decision points which could be distilled down to (1) public RFP-like process, (2) mass inquiry phase, (3) NDA/due diligence phase, (4) finalists named publicly, and finally (5) award.

Fast forward to late 2025 when Atlanta is awarded team 17 in the NWSL mix. While there was quite, grassroots interest and private communications years prior to the awarding, there was quite literally _2 days_ between when interest broke to the public broke and the award was announced. If you take into account in September 2025 when [Bremer announced the switch to rolling expansion](https://www.sportsbusinessjournal.com/Articles/2025/09/18/nwsl-shifts-to-rolling-expansion-process-with-undefined-timeline/) to the November 2025 announcement -- it's interesting to point out that they follow roughly the same eight week timeline.
